# Email Template - Collapsing Sections

Below is a modular email template.  
Expand each component to see structure, examples, and usage notes.

---

<details>
  <summary><strong>1. Subject Line</strong></summary>

### Purpose
The subject determines whether your email gets opened.  
It must be clear, concise, and immediately relevant.

### Examples
- Request for meeting: “Request: 20-minute meeting about X”
- Follow-up: “Following up on our discussion”
- Update: “Update: Completed phase 1 of the project”
- Introduction: “Introduction — Recommended by [Name]”

### Guidelines
- Keep it under 60 characters
- Place the most important keyword first
- Avoid vague subjects like “Quick question”
</details>

---

<details>
  <summary><strong>2. Greeting</strong></summary>

### Purpose
Set the tone and context based on relationship.

### Examples
- Formal: “Dear Dr. Jensen,”
- Neutral: “Hello Maria,”
- Friendly/peer: “Hi team,”

### Guidelines
- Match the recipient’s level of formality
- Avoid gendered greetings unless certain
</details>

---

<details>
  <summary><strong>3. Opening Line</strong></summary>

### Purpose
Orient the reader. Give context in the first sentence.

### Patterns
- Reminder of relationship: “I hope you’ve been well. We met at…”
- Immediate relevance: “I’m reaching out regarding…”
- Follow-up context: “As discussed last week…”

### Guidelines
- Avoid filler (“I hope this email finds you well”) unless appropriate
- Make the purpose clear within one sentence
</details>

---

<details>
  <summary><strong>4. Core Message</strong></summary>

### Purpose
Deliver the main content: request, update, response, or explanation.

### Structure
- What you need
- Why it matters
- Any relevant context
- What the reader needs to know to act

### Examples
- Request: “Could you review the attached document by Friday?”
- Update: “We’ve completed the initial analysis and found X.”
- Clarification: “To proceed, we need confirmation on Y.”

### Guidelines
- Keep paragraphs short (max 3 sentences)
- Use bullets if more than 2 items
</details>

---

<details>
  <summary><strong>5. Call to Action</strong></summary>

### Purpose
Tell the reader exactly what you want them to do next.

### Examples
- “Could we schedule a 20-minute call this week?”
- “Please confirm whether this timeline works.”
- “Let me know if we should proceed with Option B.”

### Guidelines
- Make the action specific
- Provide a deadline if appropriate
- Keep it polite, not demanding
</details>

---

<details>
  <summary><strong>6. Closing</strong></summary>

### Purpose
Finish the email with clarity and professionalism.

### Examples
- Standard: “Thank you in advance for your help.”
- Warm: “Appreciate your time and support.”
- Formal: “Thank you for your consideration.”

### Guidelines
- Avoid redundant closings (“Thanks again”) unless appropriate
</details>

---

<details>
  <summary><strong>7. Signature</strong></summary>

### Purpose
Give the recipient a quick profile of who you are and how to contact you.

### Components
- Full name
- Title / role
- Organization
- Email / phone (optional)
- URL or LinkedIn (optional)

### Example
Wayne Kirk Schmidt  
Researcher & IT Architect  
[LinkedIn URL]  
wayne.kirk.schmidt@example.com
</details>

---

<details>
  <summary><strong>8. Optional Components</strong></summary>

### Attachments
- Mention them explicitly: “Attached is the report…”

### Footnotes
- Use only for legal disclaimers or references.

### “P.S.” Line
- Use for urgency or a secondary note.
- High impact in marketing emails.
</details>
